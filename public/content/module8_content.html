Slide 1 – From Pilot to Practice: The Hardest Part of AI

Most AI failures in healthcare don't happen because the algorithm is wrong.
They happen because the implementation is wrong.

A technically excellent AI tool can fail to improve — or even worsen — patient care if the people, processes, and systems around it aren't ready.

This module focuses on the human and organisational dimension of AI: how to implement it well, lead through the change, and know when something has gone wrong.

Slide 2 – The AI Deployment Lifecycle

Deploying an AI tool in a clinical setting is not a single event — it's a process with distinct phases:

  • 1. Problem definition: Is there a genuine unmet clinical need? Is AI the right solution?
  • 2. Procurement and evaluation: Does this tool have adequate evidence? Does it work for our patient population? (See Module 5)
  • 3. Governance approval: Does it meet regulatory and institutional requirements? (See Module 6)
  • 4. Technical integration: Can it be connected to our EHR, imaging system, or workflow?
  • 5. Pilot phase: Test in a controlled environment with a small group of users and robust monitoring.
  • 6. Full deployment: Roll out with training, support, and monitoring in place.
  • 7. Post-deployment monitoring: Ongoing performance tracking, incident response, and scheduled review.

💡 Key insight: Most organisations underinvest in phases 5–7. A tool that works at launch can degrade over time as patient populations, clinical practices, and data patterns change.

Slide 3 – Why Workflow Redesign Is Non-Negotiable

You cannot add an AI tool to an existing workflow without changing the workflow.

Every AI tool produces an output — a recommendation, a flag, a score, an alert. Someone has to receive that output, at the right time, in the right format, with enough context to act on it.

Common workflow failures:

  • Alert fatigue: An AI that fires too many alerts gets ignored — even when it's right
  • Wrong recipient: The alert goes to a busy registrar who lacks the authority to act on it
  • Wrong timing: The recommendation arrives after the decision has already been made
  • Poor integration: The clinician has to leave their usual workflow to check the AI tool separately, so they don't bother

📋 Before deploying any AI tool, map the current workflow and redesign it to accommodate the tool's output at the right moment, for the right person.

Slide 4 – Stakeholder Engagement: Who Needs to Be Involved?

AI implementation affects more people than just the end users. A successful deployment requires:

  • Clinical staff: The doctors, nurses, and allied health professionals who will use the tool. Their workflow understanding is irreplaceable. Their buy-in is essential.
  • IT and informatics: Who will integrate the AI into the EHR? Who will maintain it? Who handles data pipelines?
  • Information governance and legal: Is the data use compliant? Are contracts with the vendor appropriate?
  • Patients and the public: Are patients informed that AI is being used in their care? Do they have an opportunity to ask questions or opt out?
  • Executives and finance: Is there a business case? Who owns the AI programme long term?
  • The vendor: They need to understand your environment — don't let them deploy and disappear.

🚩 A common mistake: Only consulting the clinical champion who championed the tool, without involving the frontline staff who will actually use it.

Slide 5 – Training and Education for Staff

Training is not a one-hour briefing. It is an ongoing process.

Effective AI training for clinical staff should cover:

  • What the tool does and doesn't do: Its intended use, its limitations, and what it was validated on
  • How to interact with it: Where outputs appear, how to interpret them, how to override
  • When to trust it and when to question it: Building appropriate (not blind) reliance
  • What to do when something seems wrong: The incident reporting pathway
  • Their professional responsibility: The AI does not replace their clinical judgement

🩺 Analogy: You wouldn't give a junior doctor a new device and say "figure it out." Training and supervised practice are essential.

Training should be refreshed when:

  • A new version of the AI is deployed
  • Performance monitoring reveals a change in AI behaviour
  • New evidence emerges about the tool's limitations

Slide 6 – The Override Culture Problem

In AI implementation, there is a risk in both directions:

  • Automation bias: Clinicians follow the AI recommendation without sufficient critical evaluation, even when their own clinical judgement would suggest otherwise.
  • Automation aversion: Clinicians reflexively ignore AI recommendations because they distrust technology, even when the evidence supports using it.

Both extremes lead to worse outcomes.

The goal is calibrated trust: engaging with AI recommendations critically, accepting them when they add value, overriding them with documented reasoning when clinical judgement differs.

💡 Practical tip: Track override rates as part of post-deployment monitoring. Very low rates suggest possible automation bias. Very high rates suggest the tool may not be fit for purpose in your setting.

Slide 7 – Post-Deployment Monitoring

Deploying an AI tool is the beginning of the responsibility, not the end.

What to monitor after go-live:

  • Performance metrics: Is the AI performing at the same level as in the validation study? Is sensitivity, specificity, or AUROC changing over time?
  • Usage patterns: Is it being used as intended? Are certain user groups not engaging with it?
  • Override rate and reasons: How often is it overridden? By whom? For what reasons?
  • Adverse events and near misses: Has the AI contributed to any incidents or near misses? Is there an incident reporting mechanism in place?
  • Dataset drift: Is the patient population or clinical practice changing in ways that might degrade AI performance?
  • User feedback: What are frontline staff saying about the tool? Their day-to-day experience is a leading indicator of problems.

Slide 8 – When to Pause or Stop an AI Deployment

Not all AI deployments succeed. Knowing when to halt a deployment is just as important as knowing how to launch one.

Signals that should trigger a formal review:

  • 🔴 A serious adverse event or near miss attributable to AI behaviour
  • 🔴 A sudden or unexplained drop in performance metrics
  • 🔴 Evidence that the AI is performing differently for different patient subgroups (e.g., worse for elderly patients or specific ethnic groups)
  • 🔴 Consistent patterns of unexpected override behaviour that suggest clinicians have lost confidence in the tool
  • 🔴 Discovery of a significant gap between the population the AI was validated on and your actual patient population

👤 Who decides: This should be a governance committee decision — not an individual clinician's. Having a clear decommissioning policy before you deploy makes it easier to act decisively when needed.

Slide 9 – Case Example: What Good Implementation Looks Like

A regional hospital deploys an AI tool to detect acute kidney injury (AKI) early using EHR data.

What they did right:

  • Ran a six-month pilot in two wards with dedicated monitoring
  • Involved nursing staff in workflow redesign — alerts went to the bedside nurse, not just the doctor
  • Tracked alert fatigue by monitoring how many alerts were actioned vs. dismissed
  • Built a feedback form into the system for staff to flag concerns
  • Set performance thresholds: if sensitivity dropped below 80%, a formal review would be triggered
  • Communicated to patients that an AI tool was being used to support their care

Outcome: Alert volumes were initially too high. The governance committee adjusted the threshold, reducing false positives by 40% while maintaining sensitivity. The tool went on to improve AKI detection rates by 22%.

✅ This didn't happen by accident — it happened because of good process.

Slide 10 – Summary: The Clinician Leader's Implementation Checklist

Whether you're leading an AI implementation or participating in one, use this framework:

  • ✅ Problem first: Is there a real clinical problem that AI can genuinely help with?
  • ✅ Evidence assessed: Has the tool been validated externally in a population like ours?
  • ✅ Governance approved: Has it gone through the appropriate institutional review?
  • ✅ Workflow mapped: Have we redesigned the workflow, not just added a layer to it?
  • ✅ Stakeholders engaged: Have frontline staff, IT, governance, and patients been involved?
  • ✅ Training planned: Is there a training programme — not just a briefing?
  • ✅ Monitoring in place: Do we have metrics, thresholds, and a review cycle?
  • ✅ Exit plan ready: Do we know when and how we would decommission this tool if needed?

AI in healthcare works when people, process, and technology are aligned. As a clinician, you are the bridge between the algorithm and the patient. That responsibility doesn't end when the tool goes live — it begins there.
