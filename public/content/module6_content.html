Slide 1 – Why Regulation and Governance Matter to You

You may not think of yourself as a regulator. But AI governance is not just a job for lawyers and compliance officers.

Every time you use, recommend, or raise a concern about an AI tool, you are participating in governance.

Understanding the regulatory landscape helps you:

  • Ask the right questions when a new AI tool is proposed
  • Know when to raise a concern and to whom
  • Protect patients and protect yourself

Slide 2 – What Is "Software as a Medical Device"?

The term SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) refers to software that performs a medical function independently of hardware.

An AI that analyses ECGs and flags arrhythmias is SaMD.
A general hospital administration system is not.

The key test: does the software directly influence a clinical decision?
If yes, it almost certainly qualifies as a medical device and requires regulatory oversight.

Slide 3 – The FDA's Framework for AI/ML-Based SaMD

The US FDA has been at the forefront of regulating AI in healthcare.

Key regulatory pathways (from Module 5 recap):

  • 510(k): Substantial equivalence to a predicate device
  • De Novo: Novel device without predicate
  • PMA: Highest-risk devices requiring clinical trial evidence

What's new with AI:

  • The FDA has released a framework for AI/ML-based SaMD, acknowledging that AI tools can change over time (unlike traditional software)
  • The concept of Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) allows manufacturers to pre-specify how the AI may be updated without requiring a new clearance for every update
  • The FDA's Digital Health Center of Excellence is developing new guidance specifically for AI tools

⚠️ Key point for clinicians: Always check that the AI version you are using is the same version that received clearance — AI tools can be updated without your knowledge.

Slide 4 – The EU AI Act: A New Layer for Healthcare AI

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (2024) introduces a risk-based framework for AI across all sectors, including healthcare.

High-risk AI systems include those used in:

  • Health and safety of individuals
  • Medical devices and clinical decision support

For high-risk AI, the EU AI Act requires:

  • Robust risk management systems
  • Training data governance and documentation
  • Transparency and disclosure to users
  • Human oversight mechanisms
  • Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity standards

🔗 Interaction with the MDR: A healthcare AI covered by the MDR must comply with both the MDR and the EU AI Act requirements. The MDR governs the device's safety and performance; the EU AI Act adds requirements around transparency, bias, and human oversight.

Slide 5 – The EU MDR in Practice

The EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745) governs medical devices including AI-powered diagnostic software.

For software, risk classification depends on the intended use:

  • Class I: Low risk (e.g., a fitness tracking app)
  • Class IIa: Moderate risk (e.g., software that supports diagnosis but a clinician still decides)
  • Class IIb: Higher risk (e.g., software used for diagnosis or monitoring of vital physiological parameters)
  • Class III: Highest risk (e.g., software intended to make life-or-death decisions)

Under the MDR, clinical evidence must demonstrate:

  • Clinical safety (no unacceptable risks)
  • Clinical performance (the tool actually works for its intended purpose)
  • Benefit-risk balance

💡 Practical implication: If a vendor claims CE marking, ask which classification, under which Directive (old MDD or new MDR), and when it was last reviewed.

Slide 6 – Hospital AI Governance Committees

Many health systems are establishing AI governance committees (also called AI ethics committees or clinical AI oversight boards).

These committees typically:

  • Review and approve AI tools before clinical deployment
  • Set standards for what evidence is required before adoption
  • Monitor AI tools post-deployment for safety and performance
  • Respond to incidents or safety signals related to AI
  • Ensure equity — that AI tools work fairly across all patient groups

👤 Your role: Know whether your institution has such a committee. If a vendor approaches your department, you should be directing them to this process — not piloting a tool independently.

Slide 7 – Audit Trails and Accountability

When an AI system influences a clinical decision, there must be a record.

Audit trails in AI systems typically capture:

  • When the AI was used (date, time, patient encounter)
  • Which version of the AI was running
  • What the AI recommended or output
  • Whether the clinician accepted or overrode the recommendation

Why this matters:

  • For safety: If a patient is harmed, you need to know whether the AI was involved and what it said
  • For learning: Patterns of AI use and override can reveal performance issues
  • For accountability: Regulators and courts may examine AI logs in complaints or litigation

🚩 Key principle: If there is no audit trail, the AI should not be used in high-stakes clinical decisions.

Slide 8 – What Institutional AI Policy Should Cover

A robust institutional AI policy should address:

  • Procurement: What approval process must a new AI tool go through before use?
  • Clinical validation: What evidence is required before deployment in your setting?
  • Transparency: Must clinicians be told when AI influenced a recommendation?
  • Patient consent: Are patients informed when AI is used in their care? Can they opt out?
  • Monitoring: Who is responsible for tracking AI performance after go-live?
  • Incident reporting: How should staff report concerns about an AI tool's behaviour?
  • Decommissioning: When and how is an underperforming AI tool taken out of service?

If your institution doesn't have a policy that covers these points, that's an important gap to flag.

Slide 9 – The Clinician's Role in the Regulatory Ecosystem

Regulators and governance committees depend on clinicians to:

  • Report safety concerns: If an AI tool behaves unexpectedly or appears to contribute to a near-miss or adverse event, report it — to your hospital governance team, and potentially to national regulators (FDA MedWatch in the US, MHRA Yellow Card in the UK).
  • Provide expert input: Regulatory agencies consult healthcare professionals when drafting AI guidance. If you have the opportunity to contribute to consultations, do so.
  • Model good practice: Every time you interrogate an AI tool before using it, discuss its limitations with colleagues, or document your reasoning when overriding an AI recommendation, you are contributing to a culture of responsible AI use.

Slide 10 – Summary: The Governance Landscape at a Glance

No single body covers everything. Governance is a layered system — and you are its last line of defence at the point of care.

  • Global – WHO AI Ethics Guidelines: Principles, not law
  • US – FDA SaMD Framework: Safety clearance and post-market
  • EU – EU AI Act + MDR: Risk classification, transparency, evidence
  • UK – MHRA / AI and Digital Regulations: Device approval and monitoring
  • Institutional – AI Governance Committee: Local approval and oversight
  • Clinical – You: Point-of-care accountability
