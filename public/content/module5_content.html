Slide 1 – Why Evaluating AI Tools Is a Clinical Skill

Every week, a new AI product promises to transform patient care.
But not every claim holds up under scrutiny.

As a healthcare professional, evaluating AI tools is now part of your clinical responsibility — just like reading a drug trial or interpreting a lab result.
This module gives you the framework to do it confidently.

Slide 2 – The Difference Between Accuracy and Usefulness

A vendor might say: "Our AI has 95% accuracy."
That sounds impressive. But ask yourself:

  • Accuracy at what task, tested on which patients?
  • Compared to what baseline?
  • In a controlled research setting or in a real ward?

A chest X-ray AI trained only on young adults may have 95% accuracy in the lab — and fail badly in your ICU full of elderly patients with co-morbidities.

🌦️ Analogy: A weather forecast claiming "90% accuracy" means nothing if it was only tested on sunny days.

Slide 3 – Key Performance Metrics You Need to Know

Four metrics matter most when evaluating AI diagnostic tools:

  • Sensitivity (Recall): How often does the AI correctly identify a real positive case?
    → High sensitivity = few missed cases (good for screening)
  • Specificity: How often does the AI correctly identify a negative case?
    → High specificity = few false alarms (good when false positives cause harm)
  • AUROC (Area Under the ROC Curve): A single number (0–1) summarising performance across all thresholds.
    → 0.5 = no better than chance; 0.9 = excellent
  • PPV / NPV (Predictive Values): The probability a positive or negative result is truly correct in your patient population.
    → These change with disease prevalence — always check.

Slide 4 – Internal vs. External Validation: Why It Matters

Internal validation: The AI is tested on data from the same source it was trained on.
→ This is the bare minimum — it does not prove generalisability.

External validation: The AI is tested on completely separate data from a different hospital, population, or time period.
→ This is what actually matters for real-world use.

Prospective validation: The AI is tested in real clinical use — the gold standard.

🚩 Red flag: If a vendor only shows internal validation results, do not assume the tool will work in your setting.

Slide 5 – The FDA Clearance Landscape (US)

In the United States, AI tools that support clinical decisions are regulated as Software as a Medical Device (SaMD).
The FDA has three main clearance pathways:

  • 510(k): The most common. Proves the device is "substantially equivalent" to an already-approved predicate device. It does not require new clinical trials.
  • De Novo: Used for novel low-to-moderate risk devices with no predicate. Requires more evidence but creates a new regulatory category.
  • PMA (Premarket Approval): For high-risk devices. Requires rigorous clinical trials — the highest bar.

⚠️ Key point: FDA clearance means the device met a regulatory standard — it does not guarantee clinical superiority or that it will work in your hospital.

Slide 6 – CE Marking and the EU MDR (European Context)

In Europe, AI medical devices are regulated under the Medical Device Regulation (EU MDR 2017/745).
CE marking signals that a device meets EU safety and performance requirements.

Key aspects relevant to AI:

  • Software is classified by risk (Class I, IIa, IIb, III)
  • Higher-risk classes require notified body review, not just self-declaration
  • Post-market surveillance and real-world performance monitoring are required
  • Clinical evidence must demonstrate actual clinical benefit, not just technical performance

👀 Watch for: Some AI tools carry CE marking for older Directives (MDD) — ask whether they've been re-certified under the stricter MDR.

Slide 7 – Red Flags in Vendor Claims

Before adopting any AI tool, watch out for these warning signs:

  • 🚩 "Our AI was validated in a landmark study" — Was it peer-reviewed? Was it funded by the vendor?
  • 🚩 "97% accuracy" — Accuracy on what dataset? Which patient population? What was the comparison?
  • 🚩 "FDA cleared" without specifying the indication — Clearance is indication-specific. A cleared retinal screening tool is not cleared for diabetic kidney disease.
  • 🚩 No published external validation — If no independent institution has validated the tool, be cautious.
  • 🚩 Opaque training data — If the vendor cannot tell you what data the model was trained on, that's a problem.
  • 🚩 No failure mode information — Every AI fails sometimes. If the vendor can't tell you when and how it fails, you cannot use it safely.

Slide 8 – The 10 Questions Every Clinician Should Ask

Before accepting any AI tool into your practice, ask:

  • What specific clinical task does this AI perform?
  • What data was it trained on — patient type, size, source, dates?
  • Has it been externally validated at institutions like mine?
  • What metrics were reported, and what do they mean in my patient population?
  • Is it regulatory-cleared (FDA / CE)? For what exact indication?
  • How does it handle edge cases or uncertain inputs?
  • What happens when it fails? Will I know?
  • How is it monitored after deployment?
  • What training and support does the vendor provide?
  • Who is responsible if it makes an error that affects patient care?

Slide 9 – Appraising an AI Research Paper

When reading a study about an AI tool, check:

  • Population: Does it match your patients in age, ethnicity, co-morbidities, and care setting?
  • Labels: Who annotated the training data? Were they experts? Was there disagreement between annotators?
  • Comparator: Is the AI being compared to a fair baseline — e.g., clinicians working with the same data, not with full clinical context?
  • Independence: Was the test set truly held out and not used in any training or tuning?
  • Conflict of interest: Who funded the study? Is the lead author an employee of the vendor?

Slide 10 – Summary: Your AI Evaluation Checklist

Every time you encounter a new AI tool, run it through this mental checklist:

  • ✅ Do I understand what it does and doesn't do?
  • ✅ Has it been validated externally, in patients like mine?
  • ✅ Are the reported metrics meaningful in my clinical context?
  • ✅ Is it regulated appropriately for this indication?
  • ✅ Do I know how it fails — and what to do when it does?
  • ✅ Is there a system for monitoring its performance over time?

If you can't answer these questions confidently, the tool is not ready for clinical use — regardless of how impressive the brochure looks.
